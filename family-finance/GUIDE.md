# The Family Money Guide — for Shlomo & Rachel

*Plain-English guidance behind the app in this folder. Education, not legal/tax/investment
advice — the two professionals you actually need are named at the bottom.*

---

## 1. "I don't know how to spend money"

That's normal for someone who spent years without it. The fix is a **system that decides
once**, so you never have to re-decide at the register:

**The Conscious Spending Plan** (Ramit Sethi) — four buckets, automated:

| Bucket | Target | What goes in it |
|---|---|---|
| Fixed costs | 50–60% | Mortgage/rent, cars, insurance, tuition, groceries |
| Investments | 10–20% | Automatic monthly, index funds, retirement accounts |
| Savings goals | 5–10% | Emergency fund, next property, simchas, big purchases |
| **Guilt-free spending** | 20–35% | **Spend it. All of it. On purpose. Zero guilt.** |

Two rules that change everything:

1. **Automate it.** Money moves to each bucket the day it lands. Willpower is not a plan.
2. **Money dials.** Pick the 1–2 things you and Rachel genuinely love — travel, hosting,
   the kids — and spend *lavishly* there. Cut mercilessly everywhere you don't care.
   Rich isn't spending on everything; it's spending big on what matters to *you two*.

For the "spending feels wrong" feeling specifically, read **Die With Zero** (Bill Perkins):
money's job is to buy memories and impact *while you're alive to see them* — including
giving to your kids in their 20s–30s when it changes their lives, not from a will at 60.

## 2. Trusts for the kids — what to actually do, in order

The first three items cost little and cover most of the risk:

1. **Wills for both of you** — this is where you name **guardians** for the kids.
   Without it, a court decides. Non-negotiable, do it this quarter.
2. **Term life insurance** on each of you — 20–30 year level term, ~10–12× annual income.
   It's cheap. If anyone pushes whole-life or "infinite banking," walk away.
3. **Six-month emergency fund** in a high-yield savings account.

Then the trust itself:

- **Revocable living trust** — the workhorse for families like yours. You keep full
  control of everything while alive (change it, spend it, dissolve it). When you're gone,
  the kids inherit **without probate**, at ages you choose (a common pattern: one-third at
  25, half the rest at 30, the balance at 35), managed by a **trustee you pick** until then.
  It also keeps your affairs private and works across the business + real estate + accounts.
- **Fund the trust.** Retitle the house and non-retirement accounts into it, and align
  every beneficiary designation. An unfunded trust is just paper — this is the step most
  people skip.
- **529 plans** — one per kid, automatic monthly contribution. Tax-free growth for
  education; many states allow K-12 tuition too. Grandparents can contribute.
- **Powers of attorney + healthcare directives** — same attorney visit, same package.

**Irrevocable trusts, SLATs, dynasty trusts** — these are estate-tax and asset-protection
tools. They matter when your estate is heading toward federal estate-tax territory (the
exemption is in the many-millions per person and scheduled amounts change with law), or
for creditor protection given you own a business. Don't DIY this — it's a one-afternoon
conversation with a specialist that either applies to you or doesn't yet.

**Cost reality:** a full package (wills, revocable trust, POAs, directives, funding
guidance) from a real estate-planning attorney typically runs **$2,500–$6,000 flat fee**.
Worth every dollar. Online templates are better than nothing but weak on trusts + a
family business.

## 3. How much into real estate?

There is no single right number. A sane framework:

- **25–40% of net worth in real estate, including your own home**, is a common healthy
  band for a family that also owns a business.
- **You already own a concentrated asset — the business.** Armada is a large, illiquid,
  undiversified position. That means the priority order of new dollars is usually:
  ① cash reserves (6 months), ② low-cost index funds (instantly diversified, liquid),
  ③ real estate, ④ more into the business only with clear ROI.
- **A rental must cash-flow from day one.** If the deal only works because "it'll
  appreciate," it's not a deal, it's a hope. Run it on today's rent, real vacancy,
  real maintenance, real management.
- **Never buy under pressure.** Good deals recur. Urgency is a sales tactic.

The app's Real Estate tab turns this into a live number: fill in the Net Worth tab, set a
target %, and it tells you how much room you have before real estate becomes too big a
share.

## 4. The books and the geniuses

Reading order — one a month, together:

1. **The Psychology of Money — Morgan Housel.** Start here. Behavior beats spreadsheets;
   "enough" is the whole game.
2. **I Will Teach You to Be Rich — Ramit Sethi.** The system in this app. His podcast
   (couples talking honestly about money) is made for you and Rachel.
3. **The Simple Path to Wealth — JL Collins.** Investing solved: low-cost index funds,
   keep buying, never panic-sell.
4. **The Little Book of Common Sense Investing — John C. Bogle.** The man who invented
   the index fund. Why fees are everything.
5. **Die With Zero — Bill Perkins.** How to *spend* — memories, experiences, giving with
   a warm hand.
6. **The Millionaire Real Estate Investor — Gary Keller.** If/when real estate becomes a
   real lane for you.
7. **The Richest Man in Babylon — George S. Clason.** Short, ancient, timeless.
8. **Poor Charlie's Almanack — Charlie Munger.** The wisdom layer; read last, reread forever.

The names behind the ideas: **Jack Bogle** (index funds, costs), **Warren Buffett &
Charlie Munger** (patience, temperament, circle of competence), **Morgan Housel**
(behavior), **Ramit Sethi** (systems + spending without guilt), **Bill Perkins** (spending
with intent). You don't need hot tips from any of them — the boring consensus *is* the edge.

## 5. The two professionals to hire (and how to not get burned)

1. **Estate-planning attorney** (licensed in your state) — for §2 above.
2. **Fee-only fiduciary financial advisor** — pays by flat fee or hourly, **never**
   commissions. Search napfa.org or "advice-only planner." One full-picture review
   session per year is plenty at first.

Red flags in anyone you talk to: pushes whole-life insurance or annuities, is paid by
commission, promises returns, creates urgency, or gets offended when you ask "how are
you compensated?"

## 6. The monthly ritual

Once a month, 30 minutes, you and Rachel, open the app:

1. Update the **Net Worth** tab (5 min).
2. Glance at the **Spending Plan** — did the buckets hold?
3. One question each: "What did money do for us this month?" and "What's one thing
   we want it to do next month?"
4. Check anything off the **Kids & Trusts** list you finished.

That ritual — not any single investment — is what makes a family good with money.
